First Things First—What is the Con-life?

You’re standing in a room surrounded by the actors who play your favorite characters on television… and you can just walk up...and speak to them about how they rock your world?

The con-life is traveling from city to city, sometimes across country, sometimes across the world, during free weekends just to work anywhere from two to four 8-12-hour days for a comic convention, where you may or may not get paid for your time, travel, or accommodations.

Soooo… Why Do It?

The first time I walked onto the hall floor of my very first convention (Dallas Walker Stalker Con, March 2015, for inquiring minds), I fell in love with the con world. Imagine being the world’s biggest Walking Dead fan, at the height of the show’s popularity, and you’re standing in a room surrounded by the actors who play your favorite characters on television… and you can just walk up (after the long line wait) and speak to them about how they rock your world? Sold!

Then I noticed all the staff in matching t-shirts walking around and inquired about how one would go about getting a job working for a comic convention. One told me how to go about applying for a position, and the rest, as they say, is history! After my first time as a convention staff member, I was absolutely hooked.

But, What Do You Do?

Since June of 2015 (minus the one year for Covid lockdown), I have worked at approximately 50 conventions. Possibly more. No, really. I have an entirely separate resume for conventions. I have worked in virtually every position one can work at a con; however, I am now exclusively a Celebrity Assistant, or what we call in the con-world, a “Handler.” Don’t be jealous, now. Being a handler definitely has its perks, but there are some cons, too.

Below, I’ve outlined the most common positions one can be assigned to at a comic convention, along with pros and cons of each.

Common Convention Positions

Line Control Duties

• Usually stationed in the celebrity signing area
• Walks back and forth through and around the line ensuring attendees are standing in a neat line
• Answers questions regarding celebrity pricing and other convention inquiries
• Sometimes assists with taking "selfies" of celebs with attendees
• Responsible for cutting the lines when celebs have to leave the signing area

Celebrity Assistant/Handler Duties

• Handling, keeping track of, and accounting for hundreds to thousands of dollars per day
• Keeping the celeb on task and on time for different activities
• Writing down names for autograph spellings and keeping tally of all sales
• Making sure celeb takes breaks and stays hydrated
• Keeping an eye on the crowd and making sure there are no "creepers"

Panel Team Duties

• Assist with seating attendees for panels
• Stationed around theater for optimal crowd watching
• Assist attendees with getting to a mic to ask questions
• Clearing the auditorium after a panel, preparing for the next
• Making sure the accessible seats are taken by ADA attendees
• Line control
• Crowd control

Registration

• Checking Attendees In
• Disbursing lanyards and badges or wristbands
• Customer Service

Celebrity Escort/Security

• Escorting Celebrities back and forth to various engagements
• Time Management
• Crowd Surveillance and management

Crowd Control/Security

• Greeter
• Sets the tone for the attendees
• Crowd direction and verification of lanyards/wristbands
• Crowd Control

Relief Team

• Visit all con workers in your assigned area to check if their clients need anything (sticky notes, markers, water, sanitizer, etc.)
• Gives breaks to con workers in all positions except handler. (usually a dedicated handler to assist handlers)
• Runner for various errands

WARNING!
Read Before You Apply!

Alert Danger GIFfrom Alert GIFs

There is no guarantee of what position you will be assigned when you apply for a convention, especially if you're a newbie. Most of the time, if you've worked for a con before, they'll position you where they know you're experienced. However, even the O.G.s like me get relegated to line control, sometimes. Either way, you usually don't find out your position until you check in for your first shift.
